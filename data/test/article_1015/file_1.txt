It is now clear that gamma-ray bursts (GRBs) offer us an exciting new viewpoint in relativistic astrophysics and cosmology. The central mechanism powering GRBs can produce enormous energies ranging from 10^49 to 10^52 erg in just a few seconds, enabling the acceleration of jets of plasma weighing as much as a planet to speeds close to that of light. The energy, duration of the initial emission, and characteristics of their host galaxies, including their positions within these galaxies, indicate that both types of GRBs, long and short, might ultimately lead to a specific outcome featuring a newly formed black hole surrounded by a very dense torus. Nonetheless, the two types of GRBs represent the results of very different evolutionary processes: long GRBs may occur from the collapse of an iron-core in a supermassive star, while short GRBs could result from the merger of two compact stars influenced by relativity. GRB afterglows present an opportunity to investigate the intergalactic medium during the reionization period by detecting a set of metal systems related to early starbursts. High-resolution observations with the UVES instrument currently provide essential details about the motion, ion state, and metal content of interstellar matter in regarded to GRB host galaxies up to a redshift to around z = 4. Proceeding gazes at optical and near-infrared spectrums stand to extend this redshift area possibly through the period of reionization.
