Halftoning for Strong Light Variations: Advances for the SPHERE and EPICS Tools  
Managing the intensity of light is essential for various scientific fields, including imaging systems, astronomical tools, and laser physics. This summary highlights recent work at ESO using halftoning, a method that converts a continuous image into a pattern of dots. Custom filters that vary in light transmission across different areas are created with a grid of metal pixels, offering precise control over local light passage. How these filters apply to fabricating an apodiser for the VLT SPHERE instrument and a mask for E-ELT EPICS is discussed. High-contrast instruments like SPHERE, the Spectro-Polarimetric High-contrast Exoplanet Research device (being built for the ESO Very Large Telescope, or VLT), and EPICS (the planet search initiative for the upcoming European Extremely Large Telescope or E-ELT) need specialized components with varied light transmission characteristics. A typical component example is a stellar coronagraph that minimizes the light contrast between a planet and its star. These subsystems aim to govern light transmission in a pupil plane (pupil apodisation) and in the instrument's focal plane (occulting mask, Sof regarded to as a low frequency filter). Continuously producing these specialized components is complex.
