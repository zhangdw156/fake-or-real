The spectra obtained from Pluto and Triton indicate that there is solid long chain carbon (solid CH4) present on their surfaces, reflected in multiple absorption features within the JHKL bands. Our new NACO results further confirm this, especially in the L-band. Across the wavelength range of 1.0–2.5 µm, the most obvious absorption features in the spectra of Pluto and Triton are associated with strong CH4 absorption near 1.16, 1.38, 1.66, 1.79, 2.20, 2.31, and 2.37 µm. However, due to the low spectral resolution of the NACO prism, the spectra do not show N2 absorption at 2.148 µm and CO absorption at 2.35 µm, which has been previously identified for both planets. The low resolution in the JHK bands also does not allow for CO2 detection in Triton's spectrum. In the 2.8–4.1 µm range, the Pluto and Triton spectra show significant absorptions around 3.3, 3.5, and 3.8 µm, linked to the vibrational transitions of CH4. Importantly, the slope of Pluto’s spectrum from 2.8 to 3.1 µm, indicative of pure CH4 ice versus conduction CH4 ice mixed in N2, is different from that of Triton. A steeper slope suggests a higher percentage of diluted methane in Triton compared to Pluto.
