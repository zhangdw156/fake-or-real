About six months after acquiring the initial images, we can already make some general observations about image reconstruction at the VLTI. The results indicate that this ESO facility can provide images with spatial resolution in the milliarcsecond range but only for bright objects. Achieving such spatial resolution is impossible for traditional single telescopes, even with future advancements like the ELT. So far, only relatively simple targets have been reconstructed, such as binary systems, uncomplicated circumstellar regions, or images with limited fields of view. The major findings were obtained in Visitor Mode, typically spanning three observing nights and involving at least three different telescope setups. While it has been said that the VLTI's speed is inadequate, approximately 25 minutes per calibrated point may be sufficient. However, the low quality of the calibrated visibilities limits the outcomes significantly. Additionally, all existing datasets lack adequate coverage in the uv-plane, especially along the North-American direction. These concerns follow the main complaints from users of VLTI and should be addressed by the Science Operations team moving forward. Presently, multiple imaging projects involving B[e] stars, young stars, and supergiants are underway. Researchers generally conclude that the reconstructed images affirm the fundamental attributes of the objects without reliance on prior models for the first time. Yet, currently, these images do not offer any additional detailed insights compared to parametric models, an obstacle that could only be surmounted by enhancing both the quantity and quality of visibility measurements. Nonetheless, modeling remains essential to establish quantitative thresholds and to evaluate the base physics of the images. Placing the VLTI within an international framework reveals that it does not yet offer the finest imaging solutions; specficically, the MIRC instrument at the CHARA array already improves to encompass four telescopes and is upgrading to six. Additionally, the VLTI currently lacks the best dynamic range capabilities; the CHARA's FLUOR instrument, despite operating with only two telescopes, exceeds in precision. In terms of limiting magnitude, both Keck-I and VLTI with Ideal Telescopes perform comparably. However, the VLTI provides blessedly unique spectral coverage ranging from 1 to 10 microns along with outstanding resolution capabilities nearing R = 12,000.
