The analysis of the SNLS third year dataset will offer the most accurate measurement to date of the dark energy causing the accelerated expansion of the universe. While this may attract significant interest, the SNLS also provides new insights into the astrophysics behind SN Ia progenitors and their explosions. So far, no findings have contradicted the conclusions drawn from SNe Ia used in cosmological studies, but some questions remain. For instance, why are the most luminous SNe Ia often found in the youngest galaxies and associated with shorter delay times? How do SNe Ia from various environments compare in climatic analyses? One intriguing idea is the presence of multiple progenitor mechanisms. Identifying essential environmental differences between delayed and prompt events is crucial for advancement. Metallic content is expected to influence SN Ia brightness and frequency; recent observations suggest this could be the case, but they need thorough confirmation through detailed spectroscopy of the larger and uniform host galaxies a survey like SNLS provides. A new program aiming for this is set to begin with the VLT. As with any experiment, the overall accuracy of the SNLS findings depends on both statistical and systematic uncertainties. As a greater number of SNe Ia participate in the analysis and the statistical errors reduce, systematically arising errors become more critical.
